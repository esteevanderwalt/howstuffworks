What's next: the frontier

You now understand the whole machine — from a loop on a GPU to a working LLM. The frontier isn't a different machine; it's pushing harder on the exact parts you've just learned. Here's where it's going, as of mid-2026.

▶ Start here: the frontier is the same machine, pushed harder

In plain words: nothing on the frontier replaces what you've learned. Every cutting-edge idea is a twist on the matmul, attention, and training loop from explainers #1–7.

That's the payoff of reading the whole series: you can now read the headlines. "Reasoning models," "mixture-of-experts," "fp8 quantization," "agents" — each is a knob on the machine you already understand. We'll tour four directions, and for each, name exactly which part of #1–7 it turns.

One honest caveat: this is the fastest-moving corner of tech. The directions below are durable; the specific models and numbers are a mid-2026 snapshot and will date quickly. The mental model won't.

1 Scaling — and now, reasoning

In plain words: for years, "make it better" meant "make it bigger." The newest twist is making it think longer at answer time instead of only training it bigger.

The last era ran on scaling laws: add parameters, data, and compute together and the loss drops predictably. That's why models — and the GPU clusters from #1 — ballooned. It hasn't stopped, but it's getting expensive, and a second axis opened up:

Inference-time compute (a.k.a. reasoning, or "test-time scaling"). Instead of only training a bigger model, you let the model think longer when answering — generating a long chain-of-thought before its final reply. OpenAI's o-series showed this works: o3 reportedly averaged ~57 million tokens of thinking per hard question. More thinking, better answers — a knob you turn at answer time, not training time.

Tie back to the series: both axes are just more matmul on more GPUs (#1–3) — one spends it during training, the other during generation (#7's loop, run much longer). Analysts now expect inference compute to dwarf training, which is reshaping the whole hardware story from explainer #1.

2 Mixture-of-Experts: huge, but cheap to run

In plain words: instead of one giant network where every weight runs for every token, split it into many "expert" sub-networks and fire only a couple per token. Enormous total size, small cost per token.

Recall the feed-forward sub-layer inside each transformer block (#7, §4). In a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model, that one network is replaced by many — say 256 experts — plus a tiny router that picks the best couple for each token. The rest stay dark. So the model holds vast knowledge in its total parameters while doing only a sliver of the work per token.

That's why MoE model cards list two numbers. DeepSeek-V3, the canonical example, is 671B total parameters but only ~37B active per token — about 1/18th firing at a time. Click to route a token and watch which experts light up:

It's the same matmuls from #3 — just applied sparsely. MoE is how much of the 2025–26 open-weight frontier (DeepSeek, Qwen, GLM, Llama 4 and others) got enormous without a proportional jump in compute.

3 Efficiency: making it cheap enough to run anywhere

In plain words: the other race isn't bigger — it's smaller and cheaper. Shrink the numbers (quantization), train small models to copy big ones (distillation), and you can run yesterday's frontier on a laptop or phone.

This direction is explainer #2's dtypes turned into a strategy:

QuantizationStore and compute weights in fewer bits — fp8, int4 instead of fp16/32 (#2, §4). Far less memory and bandwidth, so the matmuls (#3) run faster and fit on smaller hardware, with surprisingly little quality loss.
DistillationTrain a small "student" model to mimic a big "teacher." You bottle much of the capability into a fraction of the parameters.
On-deviceThe payoff: capable models (Gemma, Nemotron Nano, and others) now run locally on phones and laptops — private, offline, instant.
Tie back: this is the same precision/throughput lever as the tensor cores and TPUs from explainer #1 (§10–11) and the dtypes from #2 — now pointed at deployment. "GPT-4-level on a laptop" is mostly an efficiency story, not a new architecture.

4 Multimodal & agents: beyond a single text turn

In plain words: the model stops being text-only and stops being a one-shot answerer. It can see, hear, and watch — and it can take actions in a loop.

Two shifts, both built on the transformer you already know:

  • Multimodal — images, audio, and video are chopped into tokens too, then fed into the same stream as text (#7). One model reasons across all of it at once. Context windows have stretched to millions of tokens, enough for whole books, codebases, or hour-long videos.
  • Agents — wrap the model in a loop where it can call tools, browse, run code, or operate a computer, observe the result, and decide the next step. The model is still predicting next tokens (#7) — but some of those tokens are actions, not just words. 2026 is widely called the year agents went mainstream.
Tie back: none of this is a new core. A picture becomes tokens; an action is a token the surrounding program executes. It's the same next-token engine from #7, given more kinds of input and a loop that lets it act.

5 Open questions

In plain words: a lot is genuinely undecided. Here are the big honest uncertainties — held lightly, since reasonable people disagree.

A grounded finale should admit what nobody knows yet:

  • How far does scaling go? Each axis (bigger, longer training, more thinking) still helps — but data, money, and energy are real limits. Whether this curve bends toward something like general intelligence or plateaus is openly debated.
  • Cost and energy. If inference compute dwarfs training, the bottleneck becomes power and chips — an economics and infrastructure problem as much as a research one.
  • Reliability. These models still confidently make things up, and "reasoning" doesn't fully fix it. Trusting them for high-stakes work is unsolved.
  • Where the value (and risk) lands. Capability, safety, jobs, concentration of power — the societal questions are at least as live as the technical ones, and worth following with the same curiosity you brought here.
